Angelfish

Angelfish are a group of freshwater and marine fish species found in a variety of habitats, including coral reefs, rivers, and streams. They are known for their striking appearances, unique behaviors, and adaptability to aquatic environments.

Taxonomy and Classification

Angelfish belong to the family Pomacanthidae (marine species) and Cichlidae (freshwater species). The most commonly recognized angelfish species are:

  • Marine Angelfish (Pomacanthidae)
  • Freshwater Angelfish (Pterophyllum genus)

Physical Characteristics

Angelfish typically have a laterally compressed body with elongated fins. Their vibrant coloration often helps them blend into coral reefs or other aquatic vegetation.

Characteristic Description
Size 6 to 12 inches (marine); 4 to 6 inches (freshwater)
Color Varies; includes shades of blue, yellow, and black
Habitat Freshwater rivers (Amazon Basin) and marine coral reefs

Behavior and Diet

Angelfish are known to exhibit territorial behavior, particularly in confined spaces. They are omnivorous, feeding on algae, small invertebrates, and even tiny fish.

Conservation

While some species are abundant, others are threatened due to habitat loss and overfishing. Conservation efforts aim to protect their natural habitats and regulate trade.
